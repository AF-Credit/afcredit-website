Bridge to Let

Bridge to let is the strategy of buying a property that standard mortgage lenders will not accept, refurbishing it to a lettable standard, then refinancing onto a buy-to-let mortgage and retaining it as an income-producing investment. The bridging loan covers the period between purchase and refinance. Here is how it works from start to finish.

Why bridging finance and not a BTL mortgage from day one?

BTL mortgage lenders require the security property to meet minimum habitable condition standards — a working kitchen, bathroom and heating system. Properties that do not meet these standards cannot be mortgaged under standard BTL criteria.

This creates an opportunity: these properties are available at a significant discount precisely because the majority of buyers cannot finance them with a standard mortgage. Bridging finance — which has no minimum condition requirement — allows an investor to purchase, refurbish, and then refinance once the property meets BTL mortgage criteria.

The three stages of bridge to let

Stage 1: Purchase with bridging finance

The investor buys the property using a bridging loan, typically at 70%–75% LTV of the current (as-is) value. The bridging loan is assessed on the day-one value — what the property is worth in its present condition, not its potential value after works.

Completion is typically 7–14 working days from enquiry for standard residential. For auction purchases, the bridging loan must complete within the 28-day (or 56-day) auction completion window.

Stage 2: Refurbishment

The investor completes the refurbishment from their own capital during the bridge term. Light refurbishment — new kitchen, bathroom, decoration, flooring, boiler — typically takes 4–12 weeks. The bridge provides the time to complete the works without the pressure of a mortgage lender's minimum condition requirements.

The refurbishment increases the property's value. The difference between the day-one purchase price and the post-refurbishment GDV (gross development value) is the value-add that makes the exit work.

Stage 3: Refinance onto a BTL mortgage

Once the refurbishment is complete and the property is habitable, the investor applies for a BTL mortgage. The BTL lender values the property at its post-refurbishment value (GDV). At 75% of the GDV, the mortgage may cover the full bridging loan repayment — and in successful deals, return some or all of the initial equity to redeploy into the next deal.

The 6-month rule

Some BTL mortgage lenders will not refinance a property the borrower has owned for less than 6 months. Check with your BTL broker before committing to a short bridge term — you may need a 6–9 month minimum term to access the full range of BTL mortgage lenders at refinance.

What makes a good bridge-to-let deal

  • Significant purchase discount — the further below GDV the purchase price, the greater the potential capital return at refinance
  • Defined refurbishment scope — light cosmetic works (kitchen, bathroom, decoration) with a realistic cost estimate and contractor lined up
  • Strong rental market — the GDV must support BTL mortgage ICR tests at current rental income levels
  • BTL mortgage eligibility confirmed — ideally a mortgage in principle from a BTL lender before the bridge is drawn
  • Contingency in the bridge term — allow extra months for contractor delays and BTL mortgage processing

Bridge to let via a limited company

Many investors implement bridge-to-let strategies via SPV limited companies. The key advantage is full deductibility of bridging and BTL mortgage interest against company profits — not subject to the Section 24 restrictions that apply to individual landlords.
