How Accurate Is a Desktop Valuation
for a Bridging Loan?

Desktop valuations are now widely accepted by bridging lenders — but how reliable are they? And when should you expect a lender to push back and ask for a physical inspection instead?

The short answer

For a standard residential property in a well-documented area, a desktop valuation is typically accurate to within 5–10% of open market value. That margin is narrow enough for most bridging lenders to lend against — which is why desktop valuations have become a mainstream alternative to physical RICS inspections for eligible property.

That said, accuracy is not uniform. It depends heavily on the property type, location, data availability, and the sophistication of the valuer's data platform. Understanding when a desktop valuation works well — and when it doesn't — will help you assess whether it's the right route for your loan.

Key facts
  • Typical accuracy: within 5–10% for standard residential property
  • Data sources cross-referenced: 8–12 per report
  • Completed in: 24–48 hours
  • Maximum LTV at AF Credit: 75% (desktop valuation bridging loans)
  • Carried out by: RICS-qualified surveyors

What is a desktop valuation?

A desktop valuation is a professional property valuation carried out remotely — without the surveyor visiting the property. Instead of a physical inspection, the surveyor analyses a combination of data sources to form a value opinion: comparable sales, listing data, EPC records, floor plans, planning history, and more.

The result is a signed RICS valuation report, produced by a qualified professional, that a lender can rely on to make a lending decision. The difference from a full survey is the method, not the qualification of the person signing it off.

What data sources does a desktop valuation use?

A well-conducted desktop valuation typically draws on 8–12 data sources, cross-referenced to form a robust value conclusion. These include:

  • Land Registry sold prices — the most authoritative record of what comparable properties have actually sold for
  • Rightmove and Zoopla listing data — current asking prices and time on market for comparable properties
  • EPC records — energy performance certificates contain floor area, construction type, and age data
  • Floor plans — either from historic listings or planning records, used to verify size
  • Planning history — extensions, conversions, and planning constraints that affect value
  • Flood risk data — Environment Agency flood zone classifications
  • Estate agent appraisals — some valuers request a current market appraisal from a local agent
  • RICS house price indices — to adjust for time-lapse between comparable sales and the valuation date
  • Aerial and street-level imagery — Google Maps and comparable tools for external condition assessment
  • Lease and title data — for leasehold properties, unexpired term and service charge history

The valuer then applies their professional judgement to weight these sources and reach a value conclusion. Higher-quality platforms use proprietary algorithms to flag where data sources diverge significantly — a signal that a physical inspection may be more appropriate.

When is a desktop valuation most accurate?

Desktop valuations perform best in situations where property data is rich, consistent, and recent. Specifically:

High data density areas

Urban and suburban areas with high transaction volumes give valuers more comparable evidence to work with. In a dense residential area with dozens of similar properties selling each year, a desktop valuation can be very precise. In a rural village where properties of the same type haven't sold for years, the same method is far less reliable.

Standard construction property

Brick-built houses and flats with standard layouts are well-served by existing datasets. Properties with unusual construction — timber frame, concrete, steel-framed, or listed buildings — require a physical inspection to identify construction-specific risks that data sources won't capture.

Good condition properties

Desktop valuations assume the property is in a condition consistent with comparable sales. If a property has been significantly neglected, damaged, or altered, the data sources won't reflect that — and the desktop value will be inflated relative to reality. A surveyor attending in person would identify this; a desktop valuer cannot.

Mid-market values

Properties in the £150,000–£1,500,000 range tend to be well-served. Below the lower end, comparable evidence is usually abundant. Above roughly £1.5–2 million, the market thins, properties become more individual, and physical inspection is nearly always required.

When does accuracy fall — and lenders push back?

Lenders won't accept a desktop valuation in circumstances where the data picture is incomplete or unreliable. The most common situations are:

Situation Why desktop accuracy suffers
Rural property, sparse sales history Few or no usable comparables; value conclusions carry wide uncertainty
Unusual or non-standard construction Data sources don't capture construction type risk; physical inspection required
Property in poor or unknown condition Desktop value assumes market-ready condition; deterioration is invisible to data
Heavily extended or converted property Planning portal may not reflect unauthorised works; size data may be wrong
High-value property (£1.5m+) Thin comparable evidence; individual features drive value and require physical assessment
Conflicting data sources Where Land Registry, EPC, and listing data give inconsistent size or age data, the valuer will escalate to physical inspection

Desktop valuation vs full RICS inspection: how different are the values?

In practice, for eligible properties, the gap between a desktop value conclusion and a subsequent RICS physical inspection is often small. Research across comparable AVM and desktop valuation platforms suggests value conclusions typically land within 5–8% of the physical inspection value for standard residential property.

The implications for lending are modest at lower LTVs. On a £400,000 property with a 5% desktop-to-physical variance, the desktop value is £380,000–£420,000. At 75% LTV, that shifts the maximum loan by £15,000–£30,000 — significant, but manageable for most bridging scenarios.

Where the gap matters most is at high LTV and high loan amounts, which is exactly why lenders apply a slightly lower maximum LTV to desktop-valued loans than to physically inspected ones.

How does the accuracy affect LTV on a bridging loan?

Bridging lenders price the additional uncertainty of a desktop valuation into their LTV cap. At AF Credit:

  • AVM (automated) valuation: up to 65% LTV
  • Desktop valuation: up to 75% LTV
  • Physical RICS inspection: up to 75% LTV

The stepped structure reflects the risk profile of each method. An AVM is fully algorithmic with no professional sign-off; a desktop involves a RICS-qualified surveyor applying professional judgement; a physical inspection additionally verifies what the data cannot capture. Each step up in accuracy allows a step up in leverage.

If your property does not qualify for a desktop valuation, a full physical inspection is likely required. If speed matters and your property is a standard residential asset in an urban or suburban area, desktop valuation may get you to drawdown 1–2 weeks faster than a physical survey — at the same rate, at up to 75% LTV. Who carries out a desktop valuation?

Despite the name, desktop valuations are not automated reports. They are produced by RICS-qualified surveyors, typically working for specialist valuation firms that have invested in proprietary data platforms to support the process.

The surveyor reviews all available data, applies professional judgement to the comparable analysis, and signs off the report personally. The report carries professional indemnity insurance in the same way a physical survey would. What differs is the method of data collection — not the qualification or accountability of the person signing it.

This is different from an AVM (automated valuation model), which is generated by an algorithm with no professional involvement. AVMs are faster and cheaper but carry more uncertainty — which is why they attract a lower maximum LTV.

How long does a desktop valuation take?

A desktop valuation is typically instructed and returned within 24–48 hours. Compare this to a full RICS inspection, which must be scheduled with the occupant or agent, attended in person, and written up — typically taking 7–14 days from instruction to report.

For bridging borrowers where speed is the priority — an auction deadline, a chain that needs to exchange — the time saving can be decisive. A lender who uses desktop valuations can often have a formal offer in place within 2–5 working days of receiving a complete application.

Frequently asked questions

How accurate is a desktop valuation?

For standard residential properties in areas with good comparable evidence, a desktop valuation is typically accurate to within 5–10% of open market value. Accuracy is higher in densely built, data-rich areas (e.g. purpose-built flats, estate housing) and lower for unusual or rural properties.

Is a desktop valuation as accurate as a full RICS survey?

Not quite. A full RICS inspection physically verifies condition, access, size, and features that data sources may miss or misrecord. A desktop valuation relies on existing records, which can contain errors. However, for a straightforward residential property with good comparable evidence, the difference in value conclusion is often small — typically within 5–8%.

What data does a desktop valuation use?

Desktop valuers draw on Land Registry sold prices, Rightmove and Zoopla listings, EPC data, floor plans, planning history, flood risk data, estate agent appraisals, and RICS house price indices — typically 8–12 data sources cross-referenced to reach a value conclusion.

When will a lender not accept a desktop valuation?

Lenders typically decline desktop valuations for properties with unusual construction, significant structural issues, properties in poor condition, rural properties with limited comparable evidence, high-value properties above £1.5–2 million, and cases where the data sources give conflicting valuations.

Does a desktop valuation affect LTV on a bridging loan?

Yes. Because a desktop valuation carries slightly more uncertainty than a physical inspection, many bridging lenders apply a lower maximum LTV on desktop valuations. At AF Credit, desktop valuation bridging loans are available up to 75% LTV — the same maximum as a full RICS inspection.

How long does a desktop valuation take?

A desktop valuation is typically completed within 24–48 hours of instruction. Combined with a fast credit decision, this means a bridging loan offer can be in place within 2–5 working days — significantly faster than a physical RICS inspection, which often takes 1–2 weeks to arrange.

Who carries out a desktop valuation for a bridging loan?

Desktop valuations are carried out by RICS-qualified surveyors, typically working for specialist valuation firms that have developed proprietary data platforms. The valuer signs off the report professionally and it carries the same professional indemnity as a physical report — the difference is the method, not the qualification.

The bottom line

For a standard residential property in a well-documented area, a desktop valuation is accurate enough to support a bridging loan at up to 75% LTV — with the speed advantage of completing in days rather than weeks.

Where it falls short is for unusual property, rural locations, high values, or properties where data records are sparse or conflicting. In those cases, a physical inspection is the right route — and a lender who understands both options will tell you clearly which applies to your situation.
