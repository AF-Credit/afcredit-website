Mortgage on a Property Without
Building Regulations: Can You Buy It?

Buying a property where works have been completed without Building Regulations approval creates challenges when applying for a mortgage. But missing approval does not automatically mean unmortgageable — and in many cases a practical solution is available.

Many buyers only discover the issue after a survey, valuation or legal review identifies that alterations have been completed without the appropriate consents. In some cases the lender will proceed; in others the application is delayed, restricted or declined altogether.

At AF Credit, we specialise in helping buyers purchase properties that mainstream lenders struggle to finance — including properties without Building Regulations approval, structural issues, short leases, non-standard construction, damp, fire damage and other mortgage-restricted assets.

Quick answer: Can you get a mortgage without Building Regulations approval?

Yes, but it depends on the type of works, when they were completed, the lender's policy and the surveyor's comments. For minor historic works, many lenders will accept indemnity insurance. For significant structural alterations, lenders typically require more — structural engineer reports, a Regularisation Certificate or both. Where a lender declines, bridging finance can complete the purchase while the issue is resolved.

What Are Building Regulations?

Building Regulations are legal standards governing construction work in England and Wales. They ensure that building works are safe, structurally sound and energy efficient. Unlike planning permission — which controls whether work is permitted — Building Regulations focus on how work is carried out.

Building Regulations vs Planning Permission

These are two separate approval processes that are frequently confused. A property can have planning permission but no Building Regulations approval, or vice versa. Both may be required for a given project. Many mortgage problems arise specifically from missing Building Regulations — even where planning permission was properly obtained.

Which Works Commonly Cause Mortgage Problems?

Loft Conversions

One of the most common issues. Without Building Regulations approval, a lender may refuse to recognise the loft space as habitable accommodation — affecting the valuation and mortgageability.

Removed Load-Bearing Walls

Structural alterations without Building Regulations approval create serious lender concerns. Evidence of proper structural support — typically a structural engineer report — is usually required.

Extensions

Extensions built without approval often trigger additional enquiries. Lenders want confidence that the structure meets minimum safety and insulation standards — which Building Regulations approval confirms.

Garage Conversions

Lenders often want confirmation that garage conversions were completed to the appropriate insulation, structural and fire safety standards — which requires Building Regulations sign-off.

Can You Get Indemnity Insurance?

Often, yes. Building Regulations indemnity insurance is commonly used where the works are historic, no enforcement action has been taken and the local authority has not been approached about the works. Indemnity insurance protects against the risk of enforcement action — but it does not confirm that the works were completed correctly or safely.

Important: Indemnity insurance ≠ quality assurance

Many buyers assume indemnity insurance confirms the works are safe. It does not. The insurance addresses the legal risk of enforcement — not whether the construction was done correctly. For structural works, lenders will often require a structural engineer's report in addition to indemnity insurance.

Can You Get Retrospective Building Regulations Approval?

In many cases, yes. A Regularisation Certificate can be obtained by applying to the local authority, which may inspect the works and require parts of the construction to be exposed before issuing approval. The process can be time-consuming but may resolve lender concerns entirely — effectively restoring the property's mortgageability.

What Happens If Your Mortgage Is Declined?

A decline does not necessarily mean the property cannot be purchased. Many lenders simply require the issue to be resolved before they will lend. Once the appropriate approvals, reports or certification have been obtained, the property may become suitable for mainstream mortgage finance — creating an opportunity for buyers using bridging finance to complete the purchase while the process is worked through.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you get a mortgage without Building Regulations approval?

Yes, depending on the nature of the works and the lender's requirements. Minor historic works with indemnity insurance are often acceptable. Significant structural alterations usually require more. See our unmortgageable property guide for a broader overview.

Will missing Building Regulations stop me getting a mortgage?

Not always. Some lenders will proceed where indemnity insurance is available and the surveyor is satisfied with the property's condition. Significant structural works without approval — particularly removed load-bearing walls or uncertified loft conversions — are more likely to cause problems.

What is a Regularisation Certificate?

A certificate issued by the local authority confirming retrospective Building Regulations approval for works completed without the appropriate consent. Once obtained, it can restore the property's mortgageability and satisfy lender requirements.

Is indemnity insurance enough for missing Building Regulations?

Sometimes — for minor or low-risk historic works. For significant structural alterations, lenders typically require structural engineer reports or a Regularisation Certificate in addition to or instead of indemnity insurance.

Can I buy a property without Building Regulations at auction?

Yes. Auction finance via a bridging loan is commonly used where traditional mortgage funding is unavailable or cannot be arranged within the 28-day deadline.
