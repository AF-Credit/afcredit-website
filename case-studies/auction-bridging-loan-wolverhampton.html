Background

When the Mortgage Lender Pulls Out After the Hammer Falls

Buying at auction is legally binding from the moment the hammer falls. The buyer typically pays 10% of the purchase price on the day and has 28 days to complete the balance. There is no cooling-off period and no right to withdraw — failure to complete means losing the deposit and potentially facing a further claim if the seller re-sells at a lower price.

This buyer had done everything right in advance. A BTL mortgage lender had issued a Decision in Principle for a property in this price bracket, and the buyer had bid to a level that worked at 75% LTV. The end-of-terrace in Wolverhampton came in at £118,000 — below the expected hammer price — and the buyer was comfortable with the numbers.

The problem emerged on day 14. The mortgage lender's surveyor had visited the property and flagged conditions that fell below the lender's minimum acceptable standard: a kitchen with no useable fittings, a boiler that required replacement, and several cosmetic issues that the underwriting policy treated as structural red flags. The mortgage was declined. The buyer had 14 days left to find £106,200 in replacement funding or lose their £11,800 deposit.

⚠
The Problem With Mortgage Lenders at Auction

Mortgage lenders apply minimum habitable condition standards — a property typically needs a working kitchen, bathroom and heating system. Properties bought at auction frequently do not meet these standards — that is often why they are at auction. The buyer in this case had used a BTL mortgage to fund the purchase, without realising that the surveyor visit would trigger a condition decline. AF Credit has no minimum condition standard for bridging loans — the assessment is based on current market value at the assessed LTV, regardless of the property's internal state.

Analysis

Why This Worked

  • No minimum condition requirement — AF Credit's lending criteria does not include a minimum habitable condition standard. The property's value was assessed at the current state; its internal condition did not affect the credit decision.
  • RICS valuation confirmed the purchase price — The auction hammer price of £118,000 was consistent with the open market value in its current condition. The valuer did not discount the value materially for the cosmetic issues.
  • 75% LTV maintained — The full BTL LTV was replicated in the bridging loan, meaning the buyer's cash contribution remained the same. No additional equity injection was required.
  • Speed was the only variable — With 14 days remaining, the window was tight but achievable for a straightforward residential freehold. The valuation, credit decision and legal process all ran on parallel tracks.
  • Exit was well-evidenced — The buyer had a clear refurbishment plan, cost estimates and a target rental yield. The BTL mortgage exit was credible and completed before the 6-month bridge expired.
