Background

When a Perfect Security Is Not Enough for a Mainstream Mortgage

The borrower had built a small property portfolio over the previous decade and owned a semi-detached house in Harborne, Birmingham, outright — no mortgage, no charges. The property had been valued recently and the market in the area was active. By any conventional measure, this was a straightforward equity release case.

The difficulty was the credit file. Two County Court Judgments — both satisfied — remained visible on the register. One had been registered four years prior for £3,400 (utility debt, resolved in full), the other three years prior for £1,900 (a dispute with a contractor that had been settled). In addition, 18 months of mortgage arrears on a now-sold buy-to-let property — incurred during a period of tenant non-payment — appeared on the credit history. All arrears had been cleared before the property was sold.

The mainstream mortgage lender applied their standard credit policy and declined. The arrears and CCJs, despite being historic and fully resolved, placed the borrower outside the automated criteria. The loan-to-value, the property quality, and the strength of the exit were irrelevant at that stage — the policy system had made its decision.

The borrower needed to move quickly. A below-market-value BTL acquisition in Solihull was time-sensitive and the vendor was not prepared to wait for an extended mortgage application process.

📋
How AF Credit Assessed the Credit Issues

The two CCJs were satisfied and three to four years old. Neither had arisen from a pattern of financial difficulty — both were discrete, isolated events that had been resolved. The mortgage arrears were linked to a specific tenant non-payment period on a property that had since been sold. None of these issues indicated ongoing financial stress. The borrower had rebuilt their position, cleared every outstanding debt, and was now asking to borrow at 50% LTV against an unencumbered property worth twice the loan amount. The credit history told one story. The actual risk told another.

Analysis

Why the Credit History Did Not Prevent the Loan

Bridging lenders assess risk differently from mainstream mortgage lenders. A credit score is one input — but it is not the only one, and for many specialist lenders it is not the most important one. The factors that ultimately determined whether this loan was possible were the security quality, the LTV, and the exit strategy — not the credit file.

  • 50% LTV provided substantial security margin — At half the property's confirmed value, a 50% buffer exists before the lender's position is at risk. Even in a falling market, the exposure was well within a range the lender could recover from enforcement if required.
  • CCJs were satisfied and historic — Both judgments had been registered more than three years before the application and had been fully paid. Neither arose from a pattern of financial difficulty. They were isolated events, not indicators of ongoing risk.
  • Arrears were linked to a specific, resolved event — The 18 months of mortgage arrears were caused by tenant non-payment on a property that had since been sold. The borrower had cleared all arrears before the sale and had maintained a clean financial position since. This context matters.
  • Unencumbered property as security — A property with no existing mortgage is the cleanest possible security position. No existing lender consent needed. No redemption required. First charge registered without complication.
  • Exit strategy was specific and time-bound — The refinance exit was not vague. As the CCJs aged past the four-year mark, the borrower fell within the criteria for several specialist BTL mortgage lenders. The timing was known, the product was identified, and the borrower's income comfortably supported the long-term mortgage.
