Background

£200,000 Gross Profit — But the Sale Didn't Complete in Time

In May 2025, the borrower purchased a residential property in Penge, South East London for £475,000. Over the following months they carried out a full refurbishment at a cost of approximately £150,000, bringing the property to a high-quality saleable standard and achieving an agreed sale price of £825,000 — a gross profit of approximately £200,000 before finance costs.

The problem was timing. The buyer began renegotiating the purchase price and raised additional enquiries, making it unclear when exchange would take place. Meanwhile, the existing refurbishment bridge was approaching maturity with no extension available. Default interest — significantly higher than the contracted rate — was about to apply.

Rather than become dependent on a single exit under time pressure, the borrower sought to refinance the existing bridge with a no valuation bridging loan: clearing the existing facility in an orderly way, avoiding default interest, and retaining both the sale and a buy-to-let refinance as credible exits over an 8-month term.

⚖️
Contractor Restriction Successfully Resolved

The title included a Land Registry restriction protecting the building contractors' beneficial interest under a Declaration of Trust. This was not a registered charge ranking ahead of a new lender — but it did require additional legal work before AF Credit could take a first legal charge. Our solicitors agreed suitable undertakings with the contractors' legal representatives, allowing the no valuation rebridge to complete without delaying funding.

Analysis

Why AF Credit Approved This No Valuation Bridging Loan

This case had several features that might give a mainstream lender pause — a renegotiating buyer, a contractor restriction on title, and a refurbishment completed just months before the rebridge application. In practice, none of these were deal-breakers once the underlying position was assessed properly.

  • Conservative gross LTV with substantial equity — At 65% of the £825,000 value, the gross loan of £535,718 left approximately £290,000 of equity in the property. This headroom made a no valuation, desktop-only approach straightforward to underwrite.
  • Live sale provided independent value confirmation — The property was already under offer at £825,000. A live buyer willing to transact — even one renegotiating — provides external confirmation of market demand that supports the desktop valuation conclusion.
  • Dual exit significantly reduced risk — Rather than relying solely on the sale, the borrower had a credible buy-to-let refinance as a secondary exit. The property was refurbished to a lettable standard. This dual exit structure reduced single-outcome risk for both borrower and lender.
  • Contractor restriction was not a registered charge — The Declaration of Trust restriction protected a beneficial interest, not a secured debt ranking ahead of AF Credit. With cooperative restriction holders and appropriate undertakings agreed between solicitors, the first legal charge was registered cleanly.
  • Desktop valuation sufficient — no surveyor required — Where a full refurbishment has just completed, a physical valuation risks a down-valuation if a surveyor is unfamiliar with the local market or the standard of finish. A desktop assessment using comparable sales avoided this risk and removed a week from the process.
  • 8-month term gave structured runway for both exits — Long enough for the sale to complete or for a BTL refinance to be arranged; short enough to keep costs proportionate to the transaction.
