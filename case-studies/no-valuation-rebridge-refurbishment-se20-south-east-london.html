Background

Running Out of Time on an Existing Bridge — With a Sale That Wasn't Quite Ready

Many borrowers reach out when their bridging loan is expiring and their planned exit hasn't quite landed. Sometimes a sale is in progress but slower than expected. Sometimes a buyer is renegotiating. Sometimes a planning or legal document is still outstanding. In each case, the pressure is the same: the existing lender's deadline arrives before the exit is ready, and the borrower needs to refinance their bridge quickly or risk a default.

In this case, the borrower had completed a full refurbishment of a residential property in South East London. The property was valued at £850,000–£900,000 and was under offer — but the buyer had requested a significant price reduction and raised a number of additional enquiries, introducing genuine uncertainty around when exchange would take place.

Rather than become dependent on a single exit that might not complete on time, the borrower sought to rebridge — redeeming the existing facility, removing the maturity deadline, and retaining both the sale and a buy-to-let refinance as viable exits over an 8-month term.

There was one structural complexity: the building contractors who carried out the refurbishment had registered a Declaration of Trust and Land Registry restriction on the title, protecting their beneficial interest in the property. This was not a registered charge ranking ahead of a new lender — but it did need to be resolved before AF Credit could take a clean first legal charge.

⚖️
How the Contractor Restriction Was Handled

A contractor's Land Registry restriction protecting a beneficial interest under a Declaration of Trust is not the same as a registered charge. It does not rank as secured debt ahead of a new lender. However, it does mean that the restriction holders' consent — or a satisfactory undertaking from solicitors — is required before a new first charge can be registered cleanly. In this case, the restriction holders had indicated they were willing to cooperate, and discussions were constructive. AF Credit's solicitors worked with both sides to agree appropriate undertakings, allowing the rebridge to proceed and AF Credit to take a first legal charge on the title.

🖥️
Why No Physical Valuation Was Required

Many lenders insist on a new physical valuation when refinancing a bridge, even where equity is strong. AF Credit did not require one here. At 53–56% LTV against a £850,000–£900,000 property, the equity cushion was substantial — approximately £370,000–£420,000. The property was already under offer, providing additional market confirmation of value. A desktop valuation, using comparable sales and existing market evidence, was sufficient to support the no valuation rebridge without the cost and delay of instructing a surveyor.

Analysis

Why This Rebridge Was Possible — and What Made It Work

On the surface, this case had several features that might cause a mainstream lender to pause: a contractor restriction on title, a buyer renegotiating the sale price, and a valuation request on a property where works had only recently completed. In practice, none of these were obstacles once the underlying position was properly understood.

  • Very conservative LTV with substantial equity — At 53–56% loan-to-value against an £850,000–£900,000 property, the equity cushion was approximately £370,000–£420,000. This level of headroom made the no valuation rebridge straightforward to underwrite on a desktop basis.
  • Sale under offer provided independent value confirmation — The fact that the property was already under offer — even with a price reduction being discussed — provided additional external confirmation of value. A live buyer willing to transact is strong evidence of market demand.
  • Dual exit removed single-outcome risk — Rather than relying solely on the sale completing, the borrower had a credible BTL refinance exit as an alternative. This dual exit strategy significantly reduced the risk profile of the rebridge.
  • Contractor restriction was not a registered charge — The Declaration of Trust restriction protected a beneficial interest — not a secured debt ranking ahead of a new lender. With cooperative restriction holders and appropriate solicitor undertakings, the restriction was resolved without blocking the rebridge.
  • Desktop valuation was sufficient at this LTV — Many refurbishment bridges require a new physical survey once works complete. At 53–56% LTV with strong comparable evidence and a live offer, a desktop assessment was sufficient. No surveyor was instructed, reducing both cost and time.
  • 8-month term gave structured runway — The term was designed to give genuine time for either exit to complete without pressure — long enough for a BTL refinance to be arranged if needed, short enough to keep the cost of the bridge proportionate.
