Background

The Probate Deadlock — and Why Bridging Is the Standard Solution

Inheritance tax and probate create a well-documented deadlock. HMRC requires the IHT liability to be settled — or substantially paid — before the grant of probate is issued. But the grant of probate is required before the executor can legally sell the estate's property. The property cannot be sold to pay the tax, because the tax must be paid before the property can be sold.

The estate here consisted primarily of a detached four-bedroom house in Sherborne, Dorset — the family home for several decades, in good condition and well presented. The sole executor and beneficiary had inherited the property outright. The estate solicitor had calculated the IHT liability at £190,000, due to HMRC within six months of the date of death.

The executor had personal savings covering roughly £80,000 of the liability but was approximately £110,000 short of the full amount. The estate's only liquid asset — a modest cash balance — had been used to fund the funeral and initial administration costs. Without a bridging solution, the executor faced HMRC late payment interest at 7.25% per annum on the outstanding balance, or an extended period of financial uncertainty.

⚖
Lending Before the Grant of Probate

Lending against an inherited property before the grant of probate requires the executor to have the legal authority to charge the estate asset. This is confirmed by the estate solicitor and set out in the will or letters of administration. AF Credit worked directly with the estate solicitor to structure the first charge and ensure it was properly registered. At 32% LTV against a confirmed £875,000 desktop valuation, the lender's position was extremely conservative throughout. The estate solicitor was experienced in probate lending — which compressed the legal timeline significantly.

Analysis

Why This Worked

  • 32% LTV gave enormous headroom — At less than a third of the confirmed value, the lender's position was well protected throughout. Even a substantial fall in Dorset property values would not have threatened the security position.
  • Desktop valuation was entirely appropriate — With 68% equity cushion and a well-located detached house in an active market, a desktop valuation provided full comfort. No surveyor visit needed, saving both time and cost.
  • Exit was legally certain — Once probate was granted, the property could be sold and the bridge repaid. This was not a speculative exit — it was a legal near-certainty. The only variable was timing.
  • Estate solicitor's involvement de-risked the legal process — A solicitor experienced in probate lending managed the charge structure, executor authority documentation, and registration. This compressed the legal timeline to under a week.
  • Property sold close to valuation — The Sherborne detached house sold at £862,000 — within 1.5% of the desktop valuation — within three weeks of listing. The exit worked exactly as modelled.
