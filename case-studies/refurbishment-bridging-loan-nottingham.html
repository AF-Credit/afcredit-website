Background

Buying Below Market Value — Why Speed Matters

Below-market-value purchase opportunities have one thing in common: they require a buyer who can commit quickly and unconditionally. A seller offering a property at a discount is typically doing so because they need certainty and speed — not because they want to spend eight weeks waiting for a mortgage offer.

The property was a mid-terraced Victorian house in Beeston, Nottingham. Three bedrooms, original features largely intact but the kitchen, bathroom and electrical installation all original — unusable by modern letting or sale standards. The windows were single-glazed throughout. The property was structurally sound but cosmetically dated throughout.

The seller, an elderly owner-occupier downsizing to a care facility, wanted a cash buyer or the equivalent — someone who could exchange and complete quickly without mortgage conditions. The borrower, an experienced local developer who had completed several similar projects in Nottingham, recognised the opportunity and made an offer of £205,000. It was accepted on the basis of a three-week completion timeline.

A standard mortgage was not viable on this timeline or against this property type — the condition would likely generate a mortgage hold-back or retention. The borrower needed a bridging lender who could complete in under three weeks against a property in need of works.

📈
Day-One Value vs GDV — How the Loan Was Sized

AF Credit lent at 75% of the day-one value — the property as it stood at purchase, not the projected post-refurbishment value. The RICS valuer confirmed £205,000 for the property in its current condition and noted comparable finished sales in the same street at £285,000–£300,000 over the previous 12 months. The loan of £153,750 (75% of £205,000) left the lender well protected even against the current value. The GDV of £290,000+ provided clear visibility of the exit — not a condition of the loan, but a strong indicator that repayment from sale proceeds was well within reach.

Analysis

Why a Single Drawdown Worked Here

Refurbishment bridging loans can involve staged drawdowns — where the lender advances additional funds as works reach defined milestones, verified by a monitoring surveyor. This is appropriate for heavy refurbishment or development, where significant new capital is deployed at each stage and the lender's risk changes materially as works progress.

For light refurbishment — where works are cosmetic, the borrower is funding them from their own capital, and the property is structurally sound — a single drawdown on day one is simpler, faster, and more cost-effective for both parties. The lender's risk does not change significantly as works progress: the property is worth £205,000 before works and £290,000+ after. At 75% of the starting value, the lender is protected throughout regardless of whether the works are complete or not.

  • Property was structurally sound — The RICS valuer confirmed no structural defects. The works required were entirely cosmetic — no underpinning, no damp treatment, no roof work, no structural alterations. This kept the risk profile of the security stable throughout.
  • Below-market-value purchase created immediate equity — The purchase at £205,000 against a comparable finished value of £290,000+ meant the borrower had meaningful equity before works even began. The bridge was already well-secured at the point of completion.
  • Experienced borrower with a proven track record — The developer had completed four similar refurbishment projects in the Nottingham market. The works schedule was realistic, the budget was specific, and the exit comparable evidence was provided at enquiry stage. This level of preparation reduces execution risk significantly.
  • Active Nottingham market supported a fast sale exit — Victorian terraced housing in Beeston is actively traded. The borrower's exit assumption — sale at £289,500–£295,000 — was well-supported by recent comparable sales and the property sold quickly once listed.

The lender commissions a RICS valuation in the property's current condition — not at the post-refurbishment GDV. This is the basis for the loan-to-value calculation. The RICS report will typically also note the estimated GDV as a reference point, but the loan is sized against the day-one value.

GDV stands for Gross Development Value — the estimated value of the property once refurbishment is complete. It is used to assess project viability and exit value. AF Credit lends at 75% of the current (day-one) value, with GDV providing comfort that the exit supports full repayment of the bridge.
